For two years we ran dbt Core orchestrated by a self-managed Airflow on a t3.medium EC2 instance. It worked. It also required someone (me) to care about it constantly.

The incident that finally pushed us to dbt Cloud: a routine EC2 patch took down our Airflow scheduler on a Thursday night before a Friday board meeting. I spent two hours on a VPN at 11pm getting it back up. The next morning I submitted the dbt Cloud trial request.

## What We Were Running

Our setup was typical for a scrappy data team:

- **dbt Core** running inside an Airflow DAG
- **Airflow** on a self-managed EC2 instance (MWAA was too expensive at our scale)
- **Slack alerts** via a custom Python function that broke every time Slack updated their API
- **Documentation** that we generated manually when we remembered to

Total ongoing maintenance: probably 3-4 hours a month on infra that had nothing to do with actual analytics.

## The Migration

Honest assessment: easier than expected.

The dbt project files themselves transferred 1:1 — no changes needed to models, tests, or macros. The migration was mostly about:

1. Setting up the dbt Cloud environments (dev/staging/prod)
2. Moving credentials into dbt Cloud's secret manager
3. Recreating our Airflow DAG triggers as dbt Cloud jobs
4. Pointing Slack notifications at the native integration

Total elapsed time: about a day of actual work spread over a week.

## What We Gained

**CI/CD on every PR.** This alone was worth the price. dbt Cloud runs `dbt build --select state:modified+` on every pull request, so broken models never make it to production. We caught three regressions in the first month that would have made it to prod under our old setup.

**Automatic documentation.** The docs site updates on every production run. We finally stopped saying "we should update the docs" because it just happens.

**Slim CI.** Running only modified models and their downstream dependencies cut our CI runtime from 18 minutes to under 4.

**No more 11pm EC2 incidents.**

## What We Lost (or Didn't)

Cost went up — but the math works out when you account for the EC2 instance, the engineering time spent on maintenance, and the incident risk. For a team of our size, self-managed Airflow was a false economy.

We do miss having full control over the orchestration layer. dbt Cloud's job scheduling is good but not as flexible as Airflow for complex dependency graphs. If we ever need cross-tool orchestration (dbt + Spark + custom Python), we'd revisit.

For now: would recommend to any team spending more than 2 hours a month babysitting their orchestration infra.
