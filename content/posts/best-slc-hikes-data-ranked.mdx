We have lived in Utah for two years and hiked 47 trails within 90 minutes of Salt Lake City. We are also data people. So naturally we built a scoring model.

Here is the methodology, the results, and the takes you did not ask for.

## The Scoring Model

We scored each trail on five dimensions (1–10 scale) with weights reflecting what we actually care about:

| Dimension | Weight | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Scenery payoff | 30% | Summit views, water features, unique geology |
| Difficulty curve | 20% | Gradual gain vs. brutal slogs |
| Crowd avoidance | 20% | Weekday vs. weekend traffic |
| Trail condition | 15% | Surface quality, signage, erosion |
| Elevation reward | 15% | Feet gained per mile |

Data sources: our own logs, AllTrails reviews (sentiment scored), and SLC County trail reports.

## Top 10 Results

**1. Mount Timpanogos via Aspen Grove — 94/100**

Nothing in Utah touches Timpanogos for the sheer combination of everything: wildflower meadows, a glacier, a summit at 11,752 feet, and views that stretch across half the state. Yes, it is long (17+ miles roundtrip). Yes, your legs will hate you. Do it anyway.

**2. Lone Peak via Jacob's Ladder — 91/100**

The most committing day hike near the city. Technical scrambling, a knife-edge ridge, and a summit that feels genuinely earned. Go early. Bring more water than you think.

**3. Pfeifferhorn via Red Pine Lake — 89/100**

The best hike most people have not done. Red Pine Lake is stunning on its own; the additional push to Pfeifferhorn delivers one of the best 360-degree views in the Wasatch. Crowds drop sharply past the lake.

**4. Grandeur Peak via Church Fork — 87/100**

The best quick-hit in the city. 3,000 feet of gain in 4 miles from a trailhead 15 minutes from downtown. Sunrise here on a weekday is one of our favorite things in Utah.

**5. Lake Blanche — 85/100**

The turquoise color of this lake in late July is legitimately unreal. Moderate difficulty with high payoff. Gets crowded on weekends — go Thursday morning.

**6. Bells Canyon Upper Falls — 83/100**

Underrated because it sits in the shadow of Lone Peak. The upper reservoir and waterfall combination punches well above its difficulty rating.

**7. Desolation Lake via Mill D North Fork — 82/100**

Long approach but the alpine basin at the top is worth every step. September is the best month — larches turning yellow, far fewer people.

**8. Mount Olympus — 80/100**

Iconic silhouette visible from the entire valley. The scramble at the top is the most fun technical terrain you will find this close to the city.

**9. Bonneville Shoreline Trail (Dry Creek Segment) — 78/100**

Our Sunday morning trail. Zero technical difficulty, wildflowers in spring, and an excuse to stop at Ruth's Diner on the way back. Different category than the others but earns its spot.

**10. Cascade Springs and Timpooneke — 76/100**

The most accessible introduction to serious Utah hiking. Paved trail to the springs, then a clear path up to alpine terrain. Perfect for visitors.

## The Methodology Caveat

Crowd scores decay fast as trails get more popular. What scored a 9 for crowd avoidance two years ago is now a 6. Check recent AllTrails reports on anything in the top five — weekend starts before 5am or you are not avoiding anyone.

Our full dataset (all 47 hikes, raw scores, and notes) is in the family data stack and gets updated after every new trail. Ask us for the DuckDB query and we will share it.
