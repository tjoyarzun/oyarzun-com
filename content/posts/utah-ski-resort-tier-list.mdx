Utah has the Greatest Snow on Earth. It also has eight distinct ski resorts within 90 minutes of Salt Lake City. After 34 days on snow across all of them this season, we have opinions — and a spreadsheet.

## The Model

We scored each resort on six dimensions. Weights reflect what matters to us as intermediate-to-advanced skiers who care about snow quality and hate waiting in lift lines:

| Dimension | Weight |
|---|---|
| Snow quality (powder days, consistency) | 25% |
| Terrain variety | 20% |
| Lift line experience | 20% |
| Accessibility from SLC | 15% |
| Apres / vibe | 10% |
| Value (lift ticket cost vs. experience) | 10% |

## The Tier List

### S Tier: Alta Ski Area

**Score: 94/100 — 6 days this season**

Alta is skiing-only (no snowboarders), which sounds gatekeep-y until you realize what it means for crowd levels and snow preservation. The terrain off Baldy and High Rustler is as good as anything in North America on a powder day. Steep, sustained, no runouts. The cult following is earned.

Lift tickets are expensive but they hit the Ikon pass, which we have. If you are not on Ikon, the value calculation changes.

### S Tier: Snowbird

**Score: 92/100 — 8 days this season**

The most vertical in Utah (3,240 feet), the most aggressively challenging terrain, and the latest closing date (sometimes into June). Snowbird and Alta share a boundary — buy the combo ticket and you have more terrain than you can explore in a week.

The tram lines on weekends are the one thing that keeps it from a perfect score. Go on weekdays or accept the wait.

### A Tier: Park City Mountain

**Score: 84/100 — 18 days this season**

Our home resort by volume because it is 35 minutes from our house and has something for everyone. The terrain variety is unmatched — 330 runs across 7,300 acres. On a non-powder day with good snow, there is no better place to spend a comfortable ski day.

The snow quality gap with Alta/Snowbird is real, though. Park City sits lower and gets different weather. What falls as powder at Snowbird sometimes arrives as heavy wet snow at Park City.

### A Tier: Deer Valley

**Score: 82/100 — 4 days this season**

Ski-only like Alta. The grooming is legitimately the best in Utah — immaculate corduroy every single morning. Deer Valley is where you go when you want a ski experience that is premium in every detail: smaller crowds, friendly staff, restaurants that are actually good.

Expensive. Worth it for a special day.

### B Tier: Brighton

**Score: 74/100 — 3 days this season**

Brighton is Big Cottonwood's fun, unpretentious resort. Good terrain, solid snow, a younger crowd. The night skiing is the best in Utah. If you are on an Ikon pass it is a great value day when Alta/Snowbird feel like too much of a production.

### B Tier: Snowbasin

**Score: 72/100 — 1 day this season**

Snowbasin surprised us. The lodge is stunning (2002 Olympics venues), the terrain is varied and uncrowded, and the Malan's Peak area is underrated. The drive (45 minutes north toward Ogden) means fewer crowds than anything in Big or Little Cottonwood. If we lived in North Salt Lake we would be here constantly.

## Methodology Notes

We tracked conditions each day in our data stack (snowfall, temperature, wind, visibility) and cross-referenced against crowd levels rated on a 1-5 scale right off the lift. Powder day scores weight snow quality at 40% instead of 25% — a legitimate powder day changes the calculus entirely.

One resort conspicuously absent from our frequent visits: **Sundance**. Beautiful resort, Robert Redford's baby, genuinely lovely vibe. Just too small for a full day of advanced skiing. Better as a half-day with visitors who are newer to the sport.
